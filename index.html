Week 1

Monday (23 June 2025)

Today I did research for the project that I am going to work on. I will be working on AI-Based Plant disease detection system. I went through several research papers and online reading materials regarding the same. The references are as follows:

  1. Tan, Mingxing, and Quoc V. Le.
    "EfficientNet: Rethinking model scaling for convolutional neural networks." ICML (2019).

    This paper introduces EfficientNet, a model family that optimally scales width, depth, and resolution together for CNNs. It achieves state-of-the-art accuracy with fewer parameters and is ideal for mobile deployment, making it suitable for applications like plant disease detection.
    View Paper

  2. Agarwal, Mohit et al.
    "ToLeD: Tomato leaf disease detection using convolution neural network." Procedia Computer Science 167 (2020): 293–301.

    This study presents a custom CNN model trained on the PlantVillage dataset to classify tomato leaf diseases with over 91% accuracy. It compares well against larger pre-trained models and shows that lighter architectures can be highly effective in agricultural diagnostics.
    View Paper

  3. Sharma, Parul et al.
    "Performance analysis of deep learning CNN models for disease detection in plants using image segmentation." Information Processing in Agriculture 7.4 (2020): 566–574.

    The authors compare full-leaf and segmented-leaf inputs in CNN-based plant disease detection. Their segmented CNN (S-CNN) model significantly outperforms others, achieving 98.6% accuracy even on unseen real-world images, emphasizing the importance of preprocessing.
    View Paper

Tuesday (24 June 2025)

Did furthur research and discussions on the same topic.

  1. Brahimi, Mohammed et al.
    "Deep learning for tomato diseases: Classification and symptoms visualization." Applied Artificial Intelligence 31.4 (2017): 299–315.

    This work trains a CNN on over 14,000 tomato leaf images and integrates symptom visualization using saliency maps. The model achieves over 99% classification accuracy and provides visual explanations for disease predictions.
    View Paper

  2. Huyen, Chip.
    "AI Engineering." O'Reilly Media, Inc. (2024).

    This book outlines the practical, engineering side of AI systems, including deployment, feedback loops, monitoring, and edge computing. It’s highly relevant for turning machine learning models into scalable applications like your mobile plant disease app.

  3. Nagpal, Vaibhav.
    "Memory-efficient deep learning models." Medium – Data Scientist’s Diary (2021).

    This blog post discusses how to build deep learning models that use less memory, focusing on architectures like MobileNet, SqueezeNet, and EfficientNet. It provides practical insights for model deployment on devices with limited compute resources.
    Read Article

Thursday (26 June 2025)

  • Continued working with the tomato disease subset of the PlantVillage dataset, which I had collected and organized earlier.
  • While analyzing the dataset, I observed that the number of images per class (i.e., per disease type) was uneven. Some disease categories had a large number of samples, while others had very few.
  • Recognized that this imbalance could lead to a biased model — one that performs well on dominant classes but poorly on underrepresented ones, resulting in inaccurate predictions.
  • Started researching how to deal with this imbalance and came across the concept of class weighting as a form of normalization during training.
  • Learnt how to assign class weights in TensorFlow so that the model gives more importance to the minority classes, helping it generalize better across all categories.
  • Implemented class weights in my training configuration — ensuring that classes with fewer images contribute more significantly to the loss function.
  • This way, the model is encouraged to pay more attention to the underrepresented classes, even if it sees fewer examples of them during training.
